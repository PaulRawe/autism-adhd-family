Understanding ADHD and Executive Function

1

What is ADHD?

Core neuroscience explained

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) is a neurodevelopmental condition affecting approximately 5-7% of children and 2.5-4% of adults worldwide. It is not a character flaw, lack of willpower, or result of poor parenting.

üß† Core Understanding

ADHD is a neurobiological condition characterized by differences in brain structure and function, particularly in areas responsible for:

  • Attention regulation and sustained focus
  • Impulse control and behavioral inhibition
  • Working memory and information processing
  • Emotional regulation and frustration tolerance
  • Time perception and planning abilities
2

Why Homework is So Hard

Executive function challenges explained

Homework represents a perfect storm of executive function challenges. It requires nearly every executive function skill that ADHD affects:

Homework Demand Executive Function Required Why It's Hard with ADHD
Starting the task Task Initiation ADHD brains struggle to activate for non-preferred tasks
Staying focused Sustained Attention Attention drifts without external stimulation
Remembering instructions Working Memory Information "drops out" of working memory quickly
Breaking down assignments Planning/Organization Difficulty seeing the steps within a larger task
Managing time Time Perception "Time blindness" makes it hard to estimate duration

The "Stop, Drop, and Breathe" Protocol

Immediate crisis intervention

Step 1: STOP the Task Immediately

  • Action: Close the books, turn over the paper, or physically remove the homework materials
  • Say: "Let's take a break right now. The homework can wait."
  • Why: Continued exposure to the stressor keeps the nervous system activated
  • Do NOT: Try to finish "just one more problem" or push through

Step 2: Physical Movement (5-10 minutes)

  • Quick walk outside or around the house
  • Jumping jacks, dance break, trampoline
  • Throwing/catching a ball
  • Push-ups against a wall

Why: Movement helps discharge stress hormones and resets the nervous system

‚ù§Ô∏è Relationship First Principle

Critical reminder: In a crisis moment, the relationship with your child is more important than the homework. A night where homework isn't finished but you maintained connection and trust is a win.

Children with ADHD often struggle with shame and feelings of inadequacy. How you respond in crisis moments either reinforces or combats that shame. Choose connection.
